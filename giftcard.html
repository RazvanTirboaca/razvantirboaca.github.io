Gift Card case study

The UX and UI design behind a digital adventure into the depth of retail innovation.

Overview

How can we mix the offline shopping experience with online super powers to create customer excitement?

Background

Baneasa Shopping City is the most popular shopping mall in Romania having 1.4M foot-traffic every month. In 2016 we built and launched CITY iLOVE, Baneasa's loyalty app for iOS and Android. With over 25k active users in less than 1 year, CITY iLOVE won numerous prizes in the fields of digital, emerging technology and innovation in retail like "2016 ICSC Solal Marketing Awards: Silver Award for EMERGING TECHNOLOGY, Berlin, Germany".

The Project: Gift Card

Allow customers to buy gift cards directly on the website and within the app. Our goal for the project was to make the checkout process fast and easy without the overhead of integrating an ecommerce engine. More so, our ambitions were to provide a 360deg user experience by offering a smart way of checking transactions after each use.

Our high level goals were to:

  1. Make it fast and easy to order online for everyone, everywhere.
  2. Give customers full control over their spendings.
  3. Create a new benefit for installing the native app.

Deadline: 3 months from 0 to launch.

My role

I led the UX/UI design & UI development. This was a team effort between Kinecto Isobar and eAD Interactive. In addition, I worked alongside a Product Manager, Project Manager, iOS developer, Android developer and 2 backend developers.

User research

The difference between assumptions and reality.

After 3 workshops with the stakeholders where we have established the business requirements, we started to research who are the customers and why do they buy gift cards.

We conducted customer and market research to drive our planning phase. As a starting point, I used all the quantitative data we had from Baneasa Shopping City and Mastercard in order to map the customer lifetime value and the average value of an order.
*TIP: the output is as accurate as the input

One of the key insight we needed to move forward was user's online shopping behavior. We conducted qualitative reasearch inside the shopping mall.
*TIP: avoid asking questions that can be answered with YES/NO

Interesting finding: 1 of 8 people has a craked screen. Make those buttons bigger!

From this research, I developed several use-cases that we used to evaluate design decisions throughout the wireframing process, to map the user journey and prepare the marketing campaigns by targeting the right user acquisition channels.

These are the key insights that defined our customer profiles:

  1. The corporate

    • +450€ order value, buys for company’s employees.
    • Multiple legal documents required.
    • Recommended channel: offline assisted by the Sales Dept.

  2. The gifter

    • 100€ order value, buys for gifting a family member or a friend.
    • Standard checkout process.
    • Recommended channel: online, mostly desktop, via SEO, AdWords and FB ads

  3. The loyalist

    • ~300€ order value, loyal shopper, buys for personal usage or for her kids.
    • Standard checkout process.
    • Recommended channel: online, mobile user, via social media and the native app

Data-driven design

The Key To Success: Be Empirical, Be Specific.

Feeling confident about the key insights we had, we were ready to proceed to the next phase. I informed the rest of the development team of the findings and started working on the user journey.

User journey map, API based architecture

Being a cross-functional team, I engaged developers very early in the design process. We worked side by side to map the user journey and API architecture. By collaborating in this step, we made sure that everybody was confident enough to take ownership and the relationship between design & code was flawless.
*TIP: I highly recommend the User Flow template for Sketch by Greg Dlubacz. Check it out

The map highlighted how the frontend and backend will work together and provided the opportunity to discover and fix bottlenecks very early in the process. This research created a deeper empathy amongst the team.

Wireframes

I designed 8 iterations through the wireframing process getting feedback from usability testing, the stakeholders and development team.

One of our high level goals was to make it easy to order online for everyone, everywhere. That meant that the checkout process had to be easily accessible from desktop to mobile, on website and native apps. More so, it had to be accessible cross-devices. The customer could start the order from the mobile app and finish it later on the desktop, from the website.

Remember when I said that I engaged developers early in the process and worked side by side to map the user journey? We analyzed all the use-cases, advantages and disadvantages of having 3 platforms to support (web, iOS and Android), and the time needed for development, and reached the decision that the right approach was to build the full feature on the website as a responsive solution. Would be enough to have only the first step on the native app to be accessible cross-devices.
